What I Learned Talking to Nearly 30 Parents About Their Kids and AI

Notes from six weeks of customer discovery, written mostly so I can be honest with myself about what I am seeing.

For the last six weeks I have done very little other than talk to parents. Nearly thirty of them, across Winnipeg, Toronto, and the Bay Area, on calls that were supposed to be thirty minutes and routinely ran past an hour because parents have a great deal to say about how their kids are doing in school.

I went in to learn whether there is room for a new kind of learning tool for kids. I came out with a fairly detailed picture of parents quietly improvising their own education systems at home, deeply ambivalent about AI, and watching their kids form opinions about this technology that are sharper than most headlines suggest.

School has become a black box

The most universal complaint was not about teachers, curriculum, or screens. It was about information. Parent after parent described having almost no idea what is happening with their child's learning between report cards.

Several parents told me they dig through school portals to find assignments because the school will not surface them any other way. What struck me was how often this was framed as grief, not a feature request. They are asking to be let back into a relationship they feel shut out of.

Parents are running shadow schools

A large share of families have built a second, informal school that runs in the evenings and on weekends. Costco workbooks. Standing homework hours at the kitchen table. Tutors kept one grade ahead of school. Daily math sessions that parents have to drag their kids through.

The cost is paid mostly in parental time and patience. Sometimes the parent hits a knowledge wall. More often they hit an emotional wall: helping their own kid turns into disappointment, tears, and a feedback loop. A tutor often works because the tutor is a third party, not because the parent is less capable.

Kids are suspicious of AI

A surprising number of kids actively dislike AI. Some refuse to use it because they worry it will weaken their ability to think. Others reject AI-generated work because it feels inauthentic. Several will only use it as an enhanced search engine.

The kids are not wrong about the failure mode. They have watched classmates use AI to avoid thinking. The dominant use case, as an answer vending machine, makes it easy to skip the cognitive work. That is not a technology problem as much as a design problem.

Repetition kills learning products

Almost every product death followed the same script. The kid is excited for two to four weeks, then the thing becomes repetitive and the kid quietly stops. Parents brought up Kumon, IXL, gamified math apps, and a graveyard of canceled subscriptions.

Duolingo came up constantly as the counterexample. Parents notice products that create durable habits. The hard part is bringing that kind of daily pull to learning without letting gamification hollow out the learning itself.

This has to reach parents directly

Schools are slow, risk-averse, and increasingly cautious about AI. Several parents described schools discouraging or punishing AI use while kids still used it in secret. That leaves no adult in the loop teaching them how to use the tool well.

The product that wins this moment is probably not the one that waits for a school board. It is the one a parent can put in front of their own kid, that the kid will actually want to use, and that teaches them to think rather than copy.

What I believe now

The bored, capable kid is one of the most underserved students in the system. Enormous energy goes toward remediation, which matters, but the kid who finishes the worksheet in five minutes and waits is often being held back by a system designed around the median.

I think the right product is Socratic, but not religiously so. It should lead a kid to the answer when leading is what they need and explain clearly when explanation is what they need. The craft is knowing which is which.

The parent has to be in the loop by design. The black box is the wound. A product that quietly shows a parent what their kid is working on and where they are growing solves the most emotionally charged problem I heard.

These six weeks convinced me the problem is real, the parents are ready, and the kids will tell you the truth if you build something honest enough to deserve it.
